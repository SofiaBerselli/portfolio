Leica Camera

Leica built its name on 100 years of optical precision. My job was to carry that into a phone, without making it feel like a phone.

Role Lead Designer
Timeline 2024 – 2025

Problem

Leica has a "Red Dot" problem: the brand reads as elitist and analog in a world of instant, AI-driven photography. Our data told a clearer story: only 42% of users took a photo within the first 7 days of downloading the app. They downloaded it for the brand and left because they couldn't replicate the Leica Look instantly. Two failure modes in one: high cognitive load in manual mode, and nothing to guide them toward success.

Vision

Fixing the drop-off wasn't a single design problem. It was three overlapping ones.

Tactile adoption Manual controls feel clunky; users abandon Pro tools >10% interaction rate with manual dials

Intentionality

I steered the product away from being a utility toward being an instructor. Two decisions drove this: interactive onboarding and invisible constraints.

The onboarding was redesigned to be animated and sequential, training the eye before putting the controls in the user's hands. I also introduced "What's New" as a recurring in-app moment to bring returning users up to speed without interrupting the shooting flow.

On the editing side, I made an unconventional call: no disabled states. A Leica user doesn't want to see what they can't do. If a specific Leica Look doesn't allow grain adjustment, we don't show a greyed-out slider. We show nothing. The "Reset Adjustments" button was placed deliberately: close enough to encourage experimentation, far enough not to be a distraction.

The LUX Grip

A true Leica experience is physical. The LUX Grip turns the iPhone into something that actually feels like a camera, and the UX had to match.

I led the design of the grip integration flow, coordinating with engineering on firmware, OS updates, and Bluetooth stability. The target: full connection in under 15 seconds. The interface mirrored Leica's physical ergonomics, so the digital controls mapped to where your thumbs already were.

The Leica Frame

The hardest brief: how do you drive subscriptions without undermining a premium brand with "Upgrade Now" popups?

My answer was the Leica Frame. Free users can access the legendary Leitz Lenses (Summilux, Noctilux), but their exports carry a branded frame. Users didn't read it as a restriction. They posted it as a badge. The social sharing it generated did more for brand awareness than any paywall conversion flow would have, and abandonment dropped 10.9%.

On the brand side, I identified a gap: early marketing assets for the LUX App looked generic. I collaborated with external photographers and agencies, overseeing model selection and colour grading to produce imagery that felt like aspirational luxury, making the app look like it deserved the red dot on the back.

Impact

Named Apple's App of the Day 2025. Free trial conversion rose 1.18% in three months through A/B testing of the paywall. Designing for a luxury brand taught me that restraint is the feature, not a compromise.

Takeaways

Taste is a design constraint as real as time or budget. Restraint isn't a style, it's a decision made for the user. Designing for a physical product taught me that the best digital interfaces borrow from the physical world: weight, resistance, intentionality. And working inside a legacy brand showed me that heritage is an asset only when the product earns it.
